# Example Bilibili Playbook

This playbook is the cognition layer for the bundled Bilibili profile.

## Audience State

The default Bilibili viewer for this profile is willing to spend time on a stronger argument, a longer setup, or a sharper case. They are less impressed by pure emotion and more interested in whether the title suggests real analysis.

They respond well to:

- a judgment that sounds earned
- a concrete case that opens into a bigger argument
- a line that signals there is more than surface-level commentary inside

They reject:

- empty provocation
- tutorial-directory phrasing
- fake density with no actual point of view
- slogan-style excitement

## What the Title Should Do

For this profile, the title should suggest discussion value.

The best lines usually:

- carry one clear judgment
- leave room for the argument to unfold
- signal that the viewer will leave with a stronger frame, not just a fact

## Anchor Split

If the brief includes anchor artifacts such as opening clip, quoted line, or cover copy:

- the title frames the larger question or judgment
- the anchor demonstrates that the discussion has weight
- the title should not consume the strongest quote before the viewer enters

## Strong Routes

- unexpected reversal for viewpoint-led videos
- specific scene for case studies and examples
- status signal for industry judgments
- identity mirror when the audience self-selects by experience level

## Failure Modes

- sounds like chapter headings
- turns a sharp argument into a generic explainer
- leans on hype instead of viewpoint
- confuses information density with clarity

## Benchmark Usage

Use the benchmark title file to compare route coverage and sharpness. If your batch feels flatter than the benchmark set, diagnose whether the problem is missing judgment, missing scene, or missing stakes.
